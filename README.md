Photoelectric

Effect, Quantum Physics, and Atomic Models

¨ I

can describe the experiment demonstrating the external photoelectric effect and

answer questions related to it.

¨ I

can explain why the external photoelectric effect is such an important

experiment and why classical wave physics reaches its limits in explaining it.

¨ I

am familiar with Einstein’s light quantum hypothesis; I can explain it and

state and apply the formulas for the energy of a photon and the work function.

¨ I

know how an electronvolt is defined, can explain when the use of this unit is

appropriate, and can apply it in practical (calculation-based) problems.

¨ I

can explain and apply the most important formulas we have learned in quantum

physics.

¨ I

can explain how spectral analysis works, describe the experimental setup, and

discuss different emission and absorption spectra.

¨ I

know what is meant by wave–particle duality; I can state in which situations

light exhibits wave properties and when it exhibits particle properties, and I

know the key characteristics of both.

¨ I

am familiar with the most important milestones in the development of atomic

models (Thomson, Rutherford, Bohr, Schrödinger) and can describe and compare

the different models.

¨ I

can explain the Rutherford gold foil experiment, interpret outcomes and describe

the conclusions for the atomic model.

¨ I

know the considerations that led to the Bohr atomic model; I understand the

quantum condition, can explain it, and know the calculations that lead to the

Bohr radius.

¨ I

know what is meant by the energy levels (energy states) of the hydrogen atom; I

can explain what they represent, know the relevant formulas, and carry out

calculations.

¨ I

can describe what happens during the absorption and emission of energy by

electrons in the hydrogen atom and perform calculations related to these

processes.

¨ I

know what is meant by orbitals, quantum numbers, and subsidiary (secondary)

quantum numbers, and I can state how the occupation of orbitals by electrons is

defined according to Wolfgang Pauli.

¨ I

can describe the relationship between the electric field and the applied

voltage in a parallel-plate capacitor and express it using the appropriate

formula (also in connection with the stopping-potential method in the

photoelectric effect).

Nuclear

Physics and Radioactivity

¨ I

know the terms mass number, atomic number, atomic mass unit, nuclides, ionizing

radiation, stable/unstable isotopes, parent isotope, daughter isotope, parent

nucleus, daughter nucleus, can explain them, and can use them correctly in

descriptions.

¨ I

can describe the four forces acting within an atomic nucleus, explain their

effects, and use this knowledge to reason about the stability of atomic nuclei.



(gravity, electromagnetic force/Coulomb force, strong interaction, weak

interaction)

¨ I

know what a nuclide chart (chart of nuclides) is, can orient myself using it,

and can read and state the most important information from it.

¨ I

know four different types of ionizing radiation and can describe and compare

their properties.



(alpha, beta⁺, beta⁻, gamma)

¨ I

can explain how radioactive radiation can be investigated, and when radiation

passes through a magnetic field, I can use the right-hand rule to determine

which type of radiation it is.

¨ I

can describe how the different types of radiation are produced and explain why

nuclear decay can occur spontaneously without external influence.

¨ I

can explain what type of radiation is dangerous to living matter and why.

¨ I

know the different types of radiation protection, what precautions must be

taken for different types of radiation, and I can assess situations and give

recommendations for appropriate behavior.

¨ I

can name various substances that emit radioactive radiation, know which type of

radiation they emit, and make considerations about the processes occurring in

the nucleus.

¨ Using

a nuclide chart, I can explain which types of decay occur in which elements and

justify these processes.

¨ I

know different decay series, can identify them, analyze them, and trace them

forward and backward (using the nuclide chart and the periodic table).

¨ I

can explain why radioactive processes follow exponential decay, describe them

mathematically, and discuss the relationship between the decay constant and

half-life/activity.

¨ I

know what is meant by the activity and half-life of a radioactive sample, can

explain these terms, and use them to make statements or predictions.

¨ Using

the half-life, I can represent the time course of the decay of a radioactive

sample.

¨ I

can explain radiometric dating (especially carbon dating), describe the

principles behind it and analyze its limits.

The

standard Model of particle Physics

¨  Understand the hierarchy of matter:



I can describe how matter is structured from large biological systems down to

fundamental particles (organs → cells → molecules → atoms → subatomic

particles).

¨ Explain elementary particles:



I can explain what an elementary particle is and how it differs from composite

particles such as protons and neutrons, which are made of quarks.

¨ Describe the structure of the Standard Model:



I can identify the three main families of elementary particles (quarks,

leptons, bosons and fermions) and distinguish between matter particles and

force carriers.

¨ Understand the structure of protons and neutrons:



I can explain how quarks combine to form protons and neutrons, and how the

fractional charges of quarks determine the total charge of these particles.

¨ Explain the fundamental forces in the Standard Model:



I can describe the roles of the strong, electromagnetic, and weak forces, and

identify their force-carrying particles (gluons, photons, W and Z bosons).

¨ Describe radioactive decay and particle transformations:



I can explain processes such as beta (+ and -) decay, including how a neutron

transforms into a proton at the quark level and the role of W bosons and charge

conservation.

¨ Understand antimatter and mass generation:



I can explain what antiparticles are, what happens during particle–antiparticle

annihilation, and describe the role of the Higgs field in giving particles

mass, as well as why gravity is not yet included in the Standard Model.
